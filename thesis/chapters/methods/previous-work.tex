\section{Related work}
\subsection{Built-in spreadsheet tools}
\label{sec:built-in-tools}
Excel and LibreOffice Calc have their own built-in tool to track dependents and precedents of a cell. If a user tracks for example the precedent of a cell (figure~\ref{fig:precedent-libre}), the program draws an arrow from the precedent to the cell. Even though the arrows highlight the precedent / dependents of a cell, if they are located on a different worksheet the user has to move to the other worksheet. Another issue is that the user only sees the precedents / dependents which are in the viewport. If they are not located in the current viewport the user also has to scroll away to locate the precedents / dependents. Both of these navigational steps can disrupt the user's spatial context in the spreadsheet and increase the overall viscosity~(definition~\ref{def:viscosity}). These costs compound with increasing complexity.

\subsection{Separate dataflow visualizations}
\subsubsection{Leveled dataflow}
\label{sec:leveled-dataflow}
Several studies have proposed different approaches to display the underlying dependency graph of spreadsheets. 
Hermans et al. present in their paper \textit{Supporting Professional Spreadsheet Users by Generating Leveled dataflow Diagrams}~\cite{hermans_supporting_2011}, a solution to explicitly show these relationships. Their approach consist of three Views: The global view, the worksheet view and the formula view. The global view (figure~\ref{fig:global-view}) shows the relationships between the worksheets. The worksheet view groups the data in a worksheet into blocks and how they relate to each other. On the lowest level is the formula view which gives more detail about the calculations of a specific formula. One of the limitations of the leveled dataflow is that it is a read only tool. It does not exist bidirectionality to allow the user to connect the data blocks to the data in the worksheet. Unlike the multiple representation spreadsheet discussed below, it does not allow edits to the view.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[scale=0.3]{chapters/methods/images/global-view.png}
    \caption{An example global view \cite{hermans_supporting_2011}}
    \label{fig:global-view}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Multiple representation spreadsheet}
\label{sec:multiple-representation}
To tackle the inability to clearly see the underlying computational structure and lack of abstraction, Sarkar et al. present their solution, multiple representation spreadsheet, in the paper \textit{Calculation View: multiple-representation editing in spreadsheets}~\cite{sarkar_calculation_2018}. Their solution leaves the grid and formula syntax untouched, but it should provide additional representation to abstract. Their additional representation is a textual view, which describes "a set of formula assignments"~\cite[p.~86]{sarkar_calculation_2018}(figure~\ref{fig:calculation-view}). The tool also allows for bidirectionality. The user can make an edit in one view, and it gets propagated to the other, it is live.
Sarkar et al. explicitly mention that solving different kinds of problems in spreadsheets requires more representations beyond their textual one~\cite[p.~91]{sarkar_calculation_2018}. Because the flow graph visualization presented in this thesis follows a very similar approach of having a side-by-side view, the tool can be understood as an additional representation. In contrast to a textual representation, where users have to mentally reconstruct the dependencies from the formula assignments, a flow graph makes these relationships explicit and improves the self-descriptiveness. On the other hand, the textual representation may be better suited for expert programmers, as the assignment syntax resembles code.

\subsection{Grid-embedded visualizations}
\subsubsection{Fluid Visualization}
Igarashi et al., in the paper \textit{Fluid Visualization of Spreadsheet Structures}~\cite{igarashi_fluid_1998}, embed the visualization directly into the spreadsheet grid. The first visualization is the transient local view, which allows the user to see the incoming and outgoing cells of a selected cell. It is called transient because the view appears and gradually fades depending on where the cursor is moved to. The second view is the static global view, which shows the entire dataflow graph at once to the user. To highlight the direction of the dataflow, the system animates the dataflow. Having the visualization directly overlaid on the spreadsheet grid reduces the context loss when switching between visualization and spreadsheet view. However, the visualization can occlude important information, similar to the built-in tools. Furthermore, the visualizations cannot display inter-worksheet dependencies.

\subsection{Summary}
The previously mentioned tools present different strategies for supporting spreadsheet users in the comprehension and interaction process, each with distinct trade-offs.
The built-in tools in Excel and LibreOffice Calc offer a lightweight way to display dependencies in spreadsheets. However, this lightweight approach has a trade-off when working with multiple worksheets. Tracing dependencies across different worksheets can disrupt the user's spatial context.
Separate dataflow visualizations address the hidden dependencies more explicitly. The leveled dataflow diagrams by Hermans et al. make the dependency relationships visible across different abstraction levels. By doing this, users get a quick overview and can inspect certain relationships in more detail on demand. However, there is no bidirectional editing between the spreadsheet and the diagrams, increasing the viscosity of acting on new insights. The multiple representation spreadsheet by Sarkar et al. addresses this by providing a textual visualization with bidirectional editing. Of all the reviewed tools, it is the most similar to the approach presented in this thesis. However, Sarkar et al. themselves argue that different problems require different representations. The flow graph visualization of this thesis could even be understood as a complementary visual representation that aims to improve self-descriptiveness where a textual representation may be less accessible.
Grid-embedded representations, such as the Fluid Visualization by Igarashi et al. overlay the visualization directly onto the spreadsheet. This avoids context loss when switching between the visualization and spreadsheet. However, with more complex workbooks it faces similar cross-worksheet limitations as the built-in-tools.
Across the reviewed approaches, none combine a persistent visualization of formula dependencies with bidirectional editing support. The system presented in this thesis aims to bridge this gap.
