The NASA-TLX results across the three spreadsheet tasks indicate that the users experience a low to moderate cognitive load when using the tool. The overall Raw TLX, an unweighted variant of the NASA Task Load Index \cite{hart2006nasa} that averages the subscales without a pairwise importance weighting, was 27.7 (SD = 11.8) on a 0-100 scale. Table~\ref{tab:nasa-tlx-subscales-table} presents the scores for all NASA-TLX dimensions across the three spreadsheet tasks, while figure~\ref{fig:raw-tlx-scores} only presents the overall Raw TLX score of each task with their respective standard deviation. This result suggest that the tool did not impose an excessive cognitive burden on the participants, even when asked relatively complex spreadsheet tasks. However, without having comparable session where the participants used only traditional spreadsheet tools, the score should be interpreted with caution, see more in section~\ref{sec:limitations}. Task 2 achieved the lowest mean score of 23.2 (SD = 15.3), while Task 1 and Task 3 both achieved the highest mean score of 30.0 (SD = 9.5 and 18.9 respectively). The frustration levels were comparatively low, with means of 20.0, 10.0 and 15.0. This could indicate that even when participants encountered difficulties that the tool was able to help them make some progress in the comprehension and that the interactions with the flow graph were intuitive. 
It is important to note that between the individual participants there was substantial variability across all tasks. For example, participant 5 consistently reported very low cognitive load (overall means of 16.0, 5.0, 8.0) whereas participant 2 reported a significantly higher load with overall means of 29.0, 47.0 and 51.0. One reason for this variability could be the difference in spreadsheet knowledge, but the small sample size does not allow for generalizability.

The UEQ+ scores indicate an overall positive user experience with the tool across different quality dimensions. Figure \ref{fig:ueq-bar-graph-2} presents the mean scores and standard deviations for each UEQ+ scale. All seven subscales received a positive mean score, ranging from 1.45 up to 1.95.

The subscores Efficiency and Visual Clarity achieving the highest scores, both with mean scores of 1.95 (SD = 0.84 and SD = 0.45 respectively). The goal of the tool was to improve the self-descriptiveness of spreadsheets and the results suggest that the tool was perceived both as practical and goal-supportive. Especially the high usefulness score (M = 1.90) combined with its high importance rating (M = 6.20 out of 7) suggests that the participants found the tool both useful and highly relevant for this type of tasks.
This outcome is encouraging, because it suggests that the tool is well received in the dimensions that matter most to these participants.
The perspicuity scale (M = 1.75), which describes how easy it is to learn and understand a tool, received the highest importance rating among all scales (M = 6.40), indicating that participants value ease of learning and understandability. The score itself is positive, but has a notable variability (SD = 0.79), with one participant rating the perspicuity of the tool substantially lower (0.50) than others. This participant also self reported the lowest spreadsheet knowledge level of 1 and noted in the custom-questions that function arguments sometimes felt like a "blackbox". This could indicate that the tool has a steeper learning curve for beginners than intermediates.
Dependability (M = 1.45) received the lowest mean score among all subscales, though it remained in the positive range. Dependability describes if the tool behaves predictably and meets user expectations. This lower score may be partially explained by observed instances during the study, in which participants tried to edit cell reference nodes values even though they are formulas and therefore read-only. This result indicates a mismatch between what users expected to be able to do and what the system actually supported. A more predictable interaction would allow the user to edit all reference nodes independently if they contain a formula or not. % TODO: cite the norman concept of gulf of execution
The aesthetic scale (M = 1.75) received the lowest importance rating (M = 4.60), suggesting that the participants cared more about if the tool helps in achieving their goal than how it looks. Nevertheless, the positive result indicates that the participants liked the aesthetics of the tool. Also the aesthetic scale has the highest variability of all subscales (SD = 0.90) which could reflect the individual preferences for visual design.
